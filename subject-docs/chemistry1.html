History of the Atom.

In the 18th and 19th centuries, scientists like Dalton, Avogadro, and Lavoisier made significant contributions. Dalton's atomic theory in the early 1800s suggested that atoms were indivisible and unique to each element. Avogadro's hypothesis distinguished between atoms and molecules, while Lavoisier's work laid the groundwork for the law of conservation of mass.

The late 19th and early 20th centuries saw pivotal discoveries. J.J. Thomson's experiments with cathode rays led to the identification of the electron, a subatomic particle. Rutherford's gold foil experiment demonstrated that atoms have a small, dense nucleus surrounded by mostly empty space. His model proposed a planetary structure with electrons orbiting the nucleus.

Niels Bohr expanded on Rutherford's model, suggesting that electrons orbit the nucleus in quantized energy levels. This laid the foundation for quantum mechanics. Later, Schrödinger's wave equation and Heisenberg's uncertainty principle revolutionized atomic theory, emphasizing the probabilistic nature of particle behavior.

In the mid-20th century, discoveries of subatomic particles like protons, neutrons, and neutrinos refined the understanding of atomic structure. The development of quantum field theory and the Standard Model in the latter half of the century further deepened our understanding of particles and fundamental forces.

Charging: Iron ore, coke (a form of coal), and limestone are loaded into the top of the furnace. The coke serves as both a fuel and a reducing agent, meaning it helps to remove oxygen from the iron ore, reducing it to iron.

CO + F2O3 → Fe + CO2

Heating: Air is blown into the bottom of the furnace at high pressure using a hot blast stove or other means. The air reacts with the coke to produce carbon monoxide, which then reacts with the iron ore to form iron oxide (Fe2O3) and carbon dioxide.

CO2 + C → CO

Reduction: The carbon monoxide produced in the previous step reduces the iron oxide to molten iron, which collects at the bottom of the furnace. The limestone acts as a flux, combining with impurities in the iron ore to form slag, which floats on top of the molten iron and is periodically tapped off.

C + O2 → CO2

Tapping: Periodically, the molten iron and slag are tapped off from the bottom of the furnace. The molten iron is then cast into molds to form pig iron, which can be further refined into steel.

*slag refers to what every byproduct is created during this process.

Cooling and Processing: The pig iron is cooled and further processed depending on its intended use. It may be further refined in a basic oxygen furnace or an electric arc furnace to produce steel with specific properties.

Basic overview of Corrosion of metals.

Rusting is the process by which iron (fe) corrodes into its original state (how it is present in nature) as Fe (III) O2. Iron rusts when it comes in contact with oxygen and water vapor.

When Iron is extracted its lattice arrangement is disturbed, and the Fe2+ ions can bond. Often with the Oxygen in the air, this creates an Anode like effect at the area where the disturbance is at. Then the entire other part of the Oxygen becomes the Cathode, this is important because it shows how to protect our stuff, that is made out of iron. (under neutral conditions or alkaline conditions)

When there are acidic conditions there is liberation of Hydrogen at anode but rust still forms like normal.

The redox equation for rust is.

Fe2+ + OH - → Fe(OH)2

Fe(OH)2 + O2 + H2O → Fe(OH)3

There are two (main) chemical reactions that take place at the cathode

  • (re)electroplating of iron
  • Iron combining with oxygen to make ferrous oxide

Protection/prevention of rust

There are three main ways

Painting - painting is just painting the metal, if the metal is painted or tightly covered there is no place for the water, oxygen or for it to be exposed to anything and get corroded.

Cathodic protection - there are two different types

  • Sacrificial anode protection
    • Where you take an anode (of a more reactive element) and stick it to the metal it is getting reacted to.
  • ICCP
    • It is essentially constant (re)electroplating at the metal cathode making it an anode by passing current through it, this resulting in the iron that has just released its electrons to be automatically replaced and it to stick back to the overall metal again, and a sacrificial anode is still used here, but the electricity is there to kick start the process.

Anodic protection

  • Anodic protection prevents metal corrosion by making it the positive electrode and applying an external current to inhibit the corrosion reaction. It's commonly used in industries to protect metal surfaces from corrosion.

In 1886, Carl Gassner obtained a German patent (No. 37,758) on a variant of the (wet) Leclanché cell, which came to be known as the dry cell because it did not have a free liquid electrolyte. Instead, the ammonium chloride was mixed with Plaster of Paris to create a paste, with a small amount of zinc chloride added in to extend the shelf life. The manganese dioxide cathode was dipped in this paste, and both were sealed in a zinc shell, which also acts as the anode. In November 1887, he obtained a U.S. patent 373,064 for the same device. A dry-battery was invented in Japan during the Meiji Era in 1887. The inventor was Sakizō Yai. However, Yai didn't have enough money to file the patent, the first patent holder of a battery in Japan was not Yai, but Takahashi Ichisaburo. Wilhelm Hellesen also invented a dry-battery in 1887 and obtained U.S. patent 439,151 in 1890.

A dry cell uses a paste electrolyte, with only enough moisture to allow current to flow. Unlike a wet cell, a dry cell can operate in any orientation without spilling, as it contains no free liquid, making it suitable for portable equipment. By comparison, the first wet cells were typically fragile glass containers with lead rods hanging from the open top and needed careful handling to avoid spillage. Lead–acid batteries did not achieve the safety and portability of the dry cell until the development of the gel battery. Wet cells have continued to be used for high-drain applications, such as starting internal combustion engines, because inhibiting the electrolyte flow tends to reduce the current capability.

A common dry cell is the zinc–carbon cell, sometimes called the dry Leclanché cell, with a nominal voltage of 1.5 volts, the same as the alkaline cell (since both use the same zinc–manganese dioxide combination).

A standard dry cell comprises a zinc anode, usually in the form of a cylindrical pot, with a carbon cathode in the form of a central rod. The electrolyte is ammonium chloride in the form of a paste next to the zinc anode. The remaining space between the electrolyte and carbon cathode is taken up by a second paste consisting of ammonium chloride and manganese dioxide, the latter acting as a depolariser. In some designs, often marketed as "heavy duty", the ammonium chloride is replaced with zinc chloride.
